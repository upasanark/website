Chapter 3

Graph Methods for Recommendations

LightGCN, NGCF, XGCN, loss functions (BPR, InfoNCE), negative sampling, edge weighting, and training at scale

From Matrix Factorization to Graph — A Unified View

Matrix Factorization

\[ \hat{R} = U \cdot V^T \]

Predict ratings from user/item latent factors

\(u_i \in \mathbb{R}^d\), \(v_j \in \mathbb{R}^d\)

Graph View

\[ u_i = \sum_{j \in \mathcal{N}(i)} \frac{1}{\sqrt{d_i} \cdot \sqrt{d_j}} \cdot v_j \]

This IS one GCN layer on a bipartite graph!

MF was graph learning before we knew it — it computes user embeddings as normalized sums of interacted item embeddings. It just couldn't go deeper than 1 hop.

LightGCN — Less is More (He et al., 2020)

Key finding: for CF, nonlinearities and transforms HURT performance

LightGCN removes: feature transformation \(W\), nonlinearity \(\sigma\), self-connections

Layer propagation rule:

\[ e_i^{(l+1)} = \sum_{j \in \mathcal{N}(i)} \frac{1}{\sqrt{|\mathcal{N}(i)|} \cdot \sqrt{|\mathcal{N}(j)|}} \cdot e_j^{(l)} \]

Final embedding (weighted layer combination):

\[ e_i = \sum_{l=0}^{L} \alpha_l \cdot e_i^{(l)} \quad \text{where } \alpha_l = \frac{1}{L+1} \]
ComponentGCNNGCFLightGCN
Weight matrix \(W\)✓✓✗
Nonlinearity \(\sigma\)✓✓✗
Self-connection✓✓✗
Layer combination✗✗✓
For collaborative filtering, the graph structure IS the signal. Learned transformations on ID embeddings just add noise. The simpler the model, the better.

LightGCN — Why Simplification Works

Theoretical Arguments

1. No meaningful node features: User/item IDs have no semantic content — only learned embeddings
2. Feature transformation is redundant: \(W \cdot e_{\text{random init}}\) = just a different random init
3. Nonlinearities break linearity of signal: CF signal propagates linearly through the graph — σ distorts it
4. Self-connections are absorbed: Layer combination \(\sum \alpha_l e^{(l)}\) already preserves the original embedding (at \(l=0\))

Equivalence: With uniform \(\alpha_l = 1/(L+1)\), the final embedding equals:

\[ e_u = \frac{1}{L+1}\left(e_u^{(0)} + \hat{A} e_u^{(0)} + \hat{A}^2 e_u^{(0)} + \cdots + \hat{A}^L e_u^{(0)}\right) \]

= average over (L+1)-hop random walk distributions from node \(u\)

Empirical Results:

MethodGowalla (Recall@20)Yelp2018Amazon-Book
GCN0.15540.05430.0326
NGCF0.15700.05790.0344
LightGCN0.18300.06490.0411
Simpler beats complex here because the task (collaborative filtering) is inherently about structure, not features. Removing noise-adding components lets the graph signal shine through.

NGCF — Neural Graph Collaborative Filtering

Wang et al., 2019 — First multi-layer GNN for CF

NGCF propagation layer:

\[ e_i^{(l+1)} = \sigma\!\left( W_1 e_i^{(l)} + \sum_{j \in \mathcal{N}(i)} \frac{1}{\sqrt{|\mathcal{N}(i)||\mathcal{N}(j)|}} \left( W_1 e_j^{(l)} + W_2 (e_j^{(l)} \odot e_i^{(l)}) \right) \right) \]

Key components:

  • \(W_1 e_j^{(l)}\): transformed neighbor embedding (standard GCN)
  • \(W_2 (e_j^{(l)} \odot e_i^{(l)})\): element-wise interaction term — captures pairwise feature affinity
  • \(\sigma\): LeakyReLU nonlinearity
  • \(W_1 e_i^{(l)}\): self-connection (preserves own features)

Why it's worse than LightGCN:

Over-parameterization: \(W_1, W_2\) per layer on ID embeddings adds learnable noise. The interaction term \(e_j \odot e_i\) is powerful but unnecessary when IDs carry no semantic meaning.
NGCF's historical importance: it proved that multi-layer GNN propagation benefits collaborative filtering. LightGCN then showed you only need the propagation — not the bells and whistles.

XGCN — Frozen Propagation (Separate Structure from Learning)

Core idea: propagate ONCE, then iteratively refine

Step 1 — Propagate (frozen, no gradient): \[ P = \left( D^{-1/2} A D^{-1/2} \right)^K \cdot E_{\text{init}} \] Graph tells you WHO is structurally similar.
Step 2 — Refine (learned): \[ E_{\text{refined}} = \text{MLP}(P; \theta) \] MLP learns WHAT to do with that structural similarity.
Step 3 — Iterate: Re-propagate refined embeddings → refine again → converge.

Computational benefit:

No backpropagation through sparse graph operations → 5-10× faster training than end-to-end GNN

Let the graph tell you WHO is similar (cheap, fixed propagation). Then learn WHAT to do with that similarity (small MLP). Separation of concerns = scalability.

Loss Functions — BPR (Bayesian Personalized Ranking)

Rendle et al., 2009 — The classic pairwise ranking loss for implicit feedback

BPR Loss:

\[ \mathcal{L}_{\text{BPR}} = -\sum_{(u, i, j)} \ln \sigma\!\left( \hat{y}_{ui} - \hat{y}_{uj} \right) + \lambda \|\Theta\|^2 \]

where:

  • \(i\) = positive item (user interacted)
  • \(j\) = negative item (sampled non-interaction)
  • \(\hat{y}_{ui} = e_u^T e_i\) (inner product score)
  • \(\sigma\) = sigmoid function

What it optimizes:

For each user \(u\): score of positive item \(i\) should be HIGHER than score of negative item \(j\).
\(\sigma(\hat{y}_{ui} - \hat{y}_{uj})\) = probability that user prefers \(i\) over \(j\)
BPR says: "I don't care about absolute scores — just that good items score higher than bad ones." It's a pairwise comparison, one triplet at a time.

Loss Functions — InfoNCE (Contrastive Loss)

From contrastive learning — listwise comparison against many negatives

InfoNCE Loss:

\[ \mathcal{L}_{\text{InfoNCE}} = -\log \frac{\exp\!\left(\text{sim}(u, i^+) / \tau\right)}{\sum_{k=0}^{K} \exp\!\left(\text{sim}(u, i_k) / \tau\right)} \]

where:

  • \(i^+\) = positive item, \(i_1, \ldots, i_K\) = negative items
  • \(\text{sim}(u, i) = e_u^T e_i / (\|e_u\| \cdot \|e_i\|)\) (cosine similarity)
  • \(\tau\) = temperature hyperparameter

Temperature \(\tau\) controls sharpness:

\(\tau\)EffectGradient behavior
Low (0.05)Sharp: focus on hard negativesStrong signal from near-misses
Medium (0.1)BalancedSmooth gradient across negatives
High (1.0)Soft: nearly uniformWeak signal, slow learning

BPR vs InfoNCE:

AspectBPRInfoNCE
ComparisonPairwise (pos vs 1 neg)Listwise (pos vs K negatives)
Negatives per step1K (typically 256-1024)
Signal strengthWeaker per stepStronger (more contrastive info)
Typical useClassic CFSelf-supervised / contrastive CF
InfoNCE = softmax classification: "which one among K+1 items is the positive?" More negatives = more information per gradient step = faster convergence.

Negative Sampling Strategies

1. Random (Uniform):
Sample uniformly from all items
+ Simple, unbiased
− Most are "easy" (user never heard of them)
2. Popularity-biased:
\(P(j) \propto d_j^{0.75}\) (smoothed degree)
+ Harder negatives (popular items user skipped)
− Biases model toward popular items
3. Hard negatives:
Items close in embedding space but not interacted
+ Strongest learning signal
− Risk of false negatives (user might like it!)
4. In-batch negatives:
Other users' positives as your negatives
+ No extra sampling, natural difficulty
− Batch size dependent, popularity bias

Expected gradient magnitude by strategy:

Random: \(\mathbb{E}[\|\nabla\|] \approx 0\) (most negatives trivially separated)

Hard: \(\mathbb{E}[\|\nabla\|] \gg 0\) (negatives near decision boundary)

Easy negatives teach nothing (gradient ≈ 0). Hard negatives risk teaching wrong (false negatives). The sweet spot is moderately hard: popular items the user chose NOT to buy.

Edge Weighting for Interaction Graphs

Not all interactions are equal

Edge weight formula:

\[ w_{ij} = \alpha_{\text{type}} \times \text{count} \times \exp\!\left(-\frac{\Delta t}{\tau_{\text{decay}}}\right) \]

where \(\Delta t\) = time since interaction, \(\tau_{\text{decay}}\) = decay constant

Interaction Type\(\alpha_{\text{type}}\)Signal Strength
Purchase5.0Strongest — user committed money
Add to Cart3.0High intent but not confirmed
Click / Search click2.0Interest signal
View / Impression1.0Weakest — may be accidental

Impact on propagation:

\[ e_u^{(l+1)} = \sum_{j \in \mathcal{N}(u)} \frac{w_{uj}}{\sqrt{\sum_k w_{uk}} \cdot \sqrt{\sum_k w_{kj}}} \cdot e_j^{(l)} \]

Heavier edges propagate MORE signal — purchases influence embeddings more than views

A purchase is a 5× stronger signal than a view. Edge weighting ensures that committed actions dominate the embedding, while weak signals contribute proportionally less.

Training at Scale — Mini-batch on Graphs

Challenge: full-batch GNN requires entire graph in memory

28M users × 3.9M items × 423M edges → cannot fit in single GPU memory

Node-wise Sampling (GraphSAGE)
Sample K neighbors per node per hop
Computation tree per target node
+ Low variance − Exponential blowup
Layer-wise Sampling (FastGCN)
Sample a fixed set of nodes per layer
+ Bounded computation − Higher variance
Subgraph Sampling (ClusterGCN)
Partition graph into clusters
Train on one subgraph per batch
+ No neighbor explosion − Loses cross-cluster edges
Historical Embeddings
Cache neighbor embeddings from prior steps
+ Full neighborhood, O(1) − Staleness issue
For million-node recommendation graphs, mini-batch training with neighbor sampling is the only practical option. The art is controlling variance while keeping memory bounded.

Quiz — Chapter 3

Q1: Why does LightGCN remove feature transformation matrices \(W\) compared to standard GCN?
A: Because user/item IDs have no meaningful features — only learned embeddings. Applying \(W\) to randomly-initialized embeddings is equivalent to a different random initialization. The graph structure alone carries the collaborative signal, so transformation adds noise without benefit.
Q2: In BPR loss, what does \(\sigma(\hat{y}_{ui} - \hat{y}_{uj})\) represent geometrically?
A: It represents the probability that user \(u\) prefers item \(i\) over item \(j\). Geometrically, it measures how much closer \(e_u\) is to \(e_i\) than to \(e_j\) in the embedding space (via inner product difference passed through sigmoid).
Q3: XGCN freezes graph propagation. What is the computational advantage over end-to-end training?
A: No backpropagation through sparse graph operations. Graph propagation (sparse matrix multiplication over millions of nodes) is expensive to differentiate. Freezing it means gradients only flow through the small MLP — making training 5-10× faster and memory-efficient.

Quiz — Chapter 3 (continued)

Q4: If \(\tau \to 0\) in InfoNCE loss, what happens to the gradient signal?
A: As \(\tau \to 0\), the softmax becomes a hard argmax — only the hardest negative (highest similarity non-positive item) receives gradient. All other negatives contribute zero gradient. This makes training unstable and sensitive to false negatives, since one mislabeled negative dominates learning.
Q5: A user has edges to items A (purchase), B (view), C (view). With \(\alpha_{\text{purchase}}=5\), \(\alpha_{\text{view}}=1\), what fraction of the aggregated message comes from item A?
A: Unnormalized weights: A=5, B=1, C=1. Total = 7.
Fraction from A = \(\frac{5}{7} \approx 0.714\) — item A contributes 71.4% of the aggregated message.
(Note: with symmetric normalization the exact fraction depends on item degrees too, but the dominance of A is clear.)
