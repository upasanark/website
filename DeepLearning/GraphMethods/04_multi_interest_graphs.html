Chapter 4

Multi-Interest & Disentangled Graph Methods

DGCF, PIMIRec, MisGCF, Personalized PageRank, explicit clustering, and normalization

The Collapse Problem

The "Average User" Fallacy

A user buys: iPhone, yoga mat, cookbook

\[e_u = \frac{1}{3}(e_{\text{electronics}} + e_{\text{sports}} + e_{\text{books}})\]

Result: embedding sits in no-man's-land — close to none of the actual interests

Multi-interest: 3 separate embeddings, each meaningful and scorable
Averaging contradicts. You don't become a jazz-rock-classical fan by averaging — you're three fans in one person.

DGCF — Disentangled Graph Collaborative Filtering

Wang et al., 2020

Core Idea

Split embedding into \(K\) independent intent channels:

\[e_u = [e_u^1;\; e_u^2;\; \ldots;\; e_u^K]\]
Routing: for each edge \((u,i)\), learn which channel it belongs to
\[p(k|u,i) = \text{softmax}(e_u^k \cdot e_i^k)\]

Iterative routing — similar to capsule networks

Channel-Specific Propagation

\[e_u^{k,(l+1)} = \sum_{i \in \mathcal{N}(u)} p(k|u,i) \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{d_u \cdot d_i}} \cdot e_i^{k,(l)}\]
Problems:
  • Channels are latent — no interpretability guarantee
  • Iterative routing inside propagation = expensive \(O(K \cdot |E|)\)
  • Routing decisions are soft, unstable during training
DGCF asks "which interest does this edge serve?" but gives an uninterpretable answer.

PIMIRec — Prototype-Based Multi-Interest

Step 1: Cluster user's interacted items into \(K\) groups
Step 2: \(\text{prototype}_k = \text{attn-weighted mean of items in cluster } k\)
Step 3: Score candidate against ALL prototypes, take max
\[\text{score}(u, i) = \max_k \left( \text{prototype}_u^k \cdot e_i \right)\]
✓ Strength: Explicit, interpretable interests
✗ Weakness: NO collaborative propagation — prototypes from user's own history only
PIMIRec knows WHAT you like but not what PEOPLE LIKE YOU like.

MisGCF — Multi-Intent Separation Post-Propagation

Architecture: Propagate First, Then Separate

Layer 1: Standard graph propagation → single embedding per user
Intent Extraction: Multi-head FFN splits into \(K\) intent vectors
\[[z_u^1, \ldots, z_u^K] = \text{MultiHeadFFN}(e_u^{\text{propagated}})\]
Layer 2: Propagate intent-separated embeddings again
Repeat: propagation → separation → propagation

Strengths & Weaknesses

✓ Gets collaborative signal + multi-interest

✗ With pre-trained embeddings, FFN distorts the original latent space
MisGCF says "let the graph teach you, then figure out your interests" — but the FFN reshaping breaks pretrained embeddings.

Personalized PageRank for Interest-Aware Propagation

Standard PageRank

\[\pi = \alpha \cdot A_{\text{norm}} \cdot \pi + (1-\alpha) \cdot e_{\text{uniform}}\]

Personalized PageRank (PPR)

\[\text{PPR}(u) = \alpha \cdot D^{-1}A \cdot \text{PPR}(u) + (1-\alpha) \cdot e_u\]

Restart from specific node → importance relative to source

Connection to APPNP: \[H^{(K)} = \alpha (D^{-\frac{1}{2}}AD^{-\frac{1}{2}}) H^{(K-1)} + (1-\alpha) H^{(0)}\]

Why for Recommendations?

Multi-hop with teleportation: prevents over-smoothing by always mixing in the source signal.

The Explicit Clustering Approach

Core Insight

Ground interests in semantic clusters of items, not latent channels

Step 1: GMM clustering of item embeddings → soft assignments
\(p(\text{cluster}_k \,|\, \text{item})\) for all \(K\) clusters
Step 2: Tag each edge with cluster identity (primary + secondary)
Step 3: Propagate PER CLUSTER — user gets separate embedding per interest

Advantages

  • ✓ Interpretable from start (cluster ≈ product category)
  • ✓ Compatible with pre-trained embeddings (no distortion)
  • ✓ Collaborative (propagation across users)
  • ✓ Variable interests per user (depends on interaction diversity)
Don't discover interests from scratch — start with known item structure and let the graph refine it.

Normalization & Re-Anchoring

The Problem

After multiple propagation layers, cluster embeddings merge (over-smoothing per cluster)

Solution: Decouple Direction & Magnitude

Direction (interest identity):

\[\hat{\mu}_{u,c} = \frac{\mu_{u,c}}{\|\mu_{u,c}\|}\]

Magnitude (importance/mass):

\[\text{mass}_{u,c} = \|\mu_{u,c}\|_{\text{original}}\]
Re-anchor: mix propagated with original pretrained embedding \[\mu_{i,c}^{(2)} = \beta \cdot \mu_{i,c}^{\text{prop}} + (1-\beta) \cdot e_i^{\text{pretrained}}\] (for primary cluster only)

Visual Comparison

Direction = what (identity).
Magnitude = how much (importance).
Separating them prevents dominant clusters from absorbing others.

Summary — Chapter 4

1. The Problem: Single embeddings average away multi-interest structure
2. DGCF: Latent channel routing — collaborative but uninterpretable and expensive
3. PIMIRec: Prototype-based — interpretable but no collaborative signal
4. MisGCF: Post-propagation separation — collaborative but distorts pretrained space
5. PPR: Teleportation-based propagation prevents over-smoothing, enables multi-hop
6. Explicit Clustering: GMM + per-cluster propagation achieves all five desiderata
7. Normalization: Direction/magnitude decoupling + re-anchoring keeps interests separated
Key takeaway: the best multi-interest approach grounds interests in known item structure (clusters) rather than discovering them from scratch (latent channels).

Quiz — Part 1

Q1: In DGCF, how does routing probability \(p(k|u,i)\) determine which channel an edge belongs to?
A: It computes \(p(k|u,i) = \text{softmax}(e_u^k \cdot e_i^k)\) — the dot product of the user and item embeddings within each channel. The channel with the highest similarity "claims" the edge (soft assignment). This means the edge contributes most to the channel where user and item are already aligned.
Q2: PIMIRec has no collaborative signal. Give a concrete example of what it misses.
A: If users who buy yoga mats also tend to buy meditation cushions, PIMIRec won't surface meditation cushions unless this user specifically bought one. It only knows about items in your own history — it can't learn from the behavior of similar users, which is the core value of collaborative filtering.
Q3: Why does MisGCF struggle with pre-trained ASIN embeddings but not random init?
A: The multi-head FFN is a nonlinear projection that reshapes the embedding space. With random init, there's no structure to preserve — the FFN can freely organize the space. But pretrained embeddings already encode meaningful semantic structure (distances, clusters). The FFN distorts these learned relationships, destroying information that took millions of samples to learn.

Quiz — Part 2

Q4: In PPR with restart \(\alpha = 0.15\), what fraction of "attention" stays within 1-hop?
A: At each step, \((1-\alpha) = 0.85\) of the probability mass is distributed to neighbors, while \(\alpha = 0.15\) teleports back. After 1 step: 85% reaches 1-hop neighbors. After 2 steps: only \(0.85 \times 0.85 = 72\%\) of the remaining mass goes further. So the majority (~85%) of attention stays within 1-hop, with exponential decay beyond. This is what prevents over-smoothing.
Q5: After normalization, user \(u\) has cluster embeddings with directions \([0.7, 0.3]\) (electronics) and \([0.2, 0.8]\) (books), and masses 0.8 and 0.2. Which interest dominates scoring?
A: Electronics dominates — it has mass 0.8 vs 0.2 for books. The direction tells us what the interest is (its position in embedding space), while the mass tells us how strongly it contributes to scoring. An item aligned with the electronics direction gets its similarity scaled by 0.8, while a books-aligned item gets scaled by only 0.2. Direction = identity, magnitude = importance weight.
