Beam Search, Constrained Generation & Inference

Decoding strategies, prefix tries, diversity via hierarchy, business rule constraints, and the full inference pipeline

Beam Search for Item Generation

Step 1: Start with top-B tokens at Level 1 (product family)
Step 2: For each beam, expand by vocabulary at next level
Step 3: Score all B×|Vl| candidates, keep top-B
Step 4: Repeat until all levels decoded

\(\text{Score}(y_{1:l}) = \sum_{k=1}^{l} \log P(y_k \mid y_{1:k-1}, \mathbf{h})\)

Keep top-B sequences by cumulative log-probability

Beam search is greedy search with B parallel hypotheses — it trades compute (B× more work per level) for better global sequences.

Prefix Trie for Constrained Generation

Construction:

Build trie from all valid item token tuples \((t_1, t_2, \ldots, t_K)\)
Each path root→leaf = one real item's token sequence
At decoding step \(l\): only allow tokens that exist as children of current prefix

Guarantee: Every completed sequence maps to ≥1 real item

Space: \(O(|\mathcal{I}| \times K)\) where \(K\) = number of levels

Lookup: \(O(1)\) per step (hash children)

The trie acts as a guardrail — the model can only "hallucinate" items that actually exist in your catalog.

Hierarchical Beam Search & Diversity

Natural Diversity from Hierarchy:

Level 1 beams spread across categories — no MMR needed!

Example: B=10

L1 beams: 4× smartphones, 3× headphones, 2× cases, 1× charger

L3 beams: mix of Apple, Android, agnostic within each

Result: diverse across product types AND ecosystems

Coarse-to-fine decoding = implicit diversity
No post-hoc re-ranking (MMR/DPP) required
Diversity is a FREE byproduct of hierarchical tokens

Constrained Generation for Business Rules

Constraints as Trie Filtering — Zero Cost at Inference

Business RuleConstraint LevelImplementation
"Only Apple products"L3 (Ecosystem)Allow only apple token at L3
"Only items under ₹5000"L5 (Price Tier)Allow only budget, mid-range at L5
"Only from campaign category"L1 (Family)Allow only headphones at L1
"Premium brands only"L4 (Brand Group)Filter trie to top-tier brands
"Gaming intent users"L6 (Use-Case)Allow only gaming at L6

Key insight: Constraints are just trie subtree selection

\(P(y_l \mid y_{1:l-1}, \mathbf{h}) \to P(y_l \mid y_{1:l-1}, \mathbf{h}) \cdot \mathbb{1}[y_l \in \text{Trie}(y_{1:l-1}) \cap \mathcal{C}]\)

Where \(\mathcal{C}\) = allowed tokens from business constraint

This is the killer feature: in discriminative models, filtering is post-hoc and wastes compute. In generative models, constraints guide generation — you never score invalid items at all.

Full-Catalog vs uni100 Evaluation

uni100 Protocol:

Sample 99 random negatives + 1 positive
Rank target among 100 candidates
Easy negatives → inflated metrics
Standard in papers (SASRec, BERT4Rec, TIGER)

Problem: Random negatives are too easy

Recall@10 under uni100 ≈ 0.85+

Recall@10 under full-catalog ≈ 0.40

Full-Catalog with Beam Search:

Decode top-B sequences (B=50 or 100)
Resolve each sequence to item(s) via trie
Rank resolved items by beam score
Check if target item appears in top-K

Advantage: Scales to any catalog size

Decode B=100 items from 45K catalog

Cost: \(O(B \times K \times |V_{max}|)\) — NOT \(O(|\mathcal{I}|)\)

GenRec's beam search is sublinear in catalog size — discriminative models must score ALL items. This is why generative retrieval scales better to million-item catalogs.

Quiz — Chapter 6

Q1: Why does beam search with B=20 at K=6 levels NOT require scoring all 45K items?
A: At each level, beam search only expands B active hypotheses by the vocabulary at that level (|Vl| ≈ 18–256). Total candidates scored: B × K × |Vmax| ≈ 20 × 6 × 256 = 30,720 scores — but across all levels combined. The output is at most B=20 complete sequences, not the full catalog. This is sublinear in |catalog|.
Q2: A prefix trie guarantees every generated sequence maps to a real item. What happens if the model assigns high probability to an invalid token path?
A: The trie masks out invalid tokens before the softmax (sets logit to −∞). The model's probability mass is redistributed among valid continuations only. The invalid path is never explored — it's as if those tokens don't exist in the vocabulary for that position.

Quiz — Chapter 6 (continued)

Q3: How does hierarchical beam search achieve diversity WITHOUT an explicit diversity objective (like MMR)?
A: At Level 1, the model distributes probability across multiple product categories (smartphones, headphones, cases). With B=10 beams, different L1 tokens survive → downstream beams specialize within each category. The tree structure forces early branching, which produces diverse final items as a natural byproduct of the coarse-to-fine decoding order.
Q4: You want to constrain recommendations to "Apple products under ₹50,000." Which levels do you constrain, and what is the computational cost of this constraint?
A: Constrain L3=apple (ecosystem) and L5=budget or mid-range (price tier). Cost: zero additional compute — you simply restrict the trie traversal to subtrees matching these constraints. The beam search runs the same algorithm with a smaller effective vocabulary at those levels. This is why generative retrieval has an inherent advantage for filtered/constrained recommendation.
